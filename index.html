The through-line

Find the slow part. Automate it. Measure the gain.

My method is the same in every operation I have run: analyze the data to find the bottleneck, design a system around the real constraints, automate the repeatable work with AI and code, then drive adoption until the team runs on it without me. A bar that needed prices before Friday got a pricing engine built from invoice data. A store that needed 43 people scheduled without breaking house rules got a constraint solver. A community of paying members got an automated content and onboarding pipeline.

I build with AI as a working partner. Cursor and Claude are part of my toolchain the way spreadsheets are. What makes the work mine is the part AI cannot do: knowing the operation well enough to encode its real rules, managing the people and vendors around the system, and being accountable for the outcome.

How I work

The rules I keep.

Data before design

Every system starts with analysis: pour prices from invoice data, schedules from demand curves, the GM case from sales numbers. If the data doesn't justify the workflow, the workflow doesn't ship.

Automate the repeatable

AI and code take the repetitive load so people can do judgment work. The gains have to be measurable: hours saved, errors removed, decisions made faster.

Adoption is the finish line

A system only counts when the team actually runs on it. That means clean handoff docs, training built in, and artifacts people can use on day one: the posted schedule, the priced menu, the board-ready report.

Honesty is the moat

Credit split with my co-founder where it's due. A repo that says "this stopped at the scaffold." Real numbers only. Overselling is a debt that always gets called, and stakeholder trust is the asset that compounds.
